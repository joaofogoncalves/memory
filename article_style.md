## Long-Form Article Style Supplement

Use this alongside `writing_style.md` (primary authority) and `profile.md` (voice patterns) when writing articles. Everything in those guides applies. This document covers what's different about long-form.

---

### What changes from LinkedIn posts

Articles are the same voice with more room. The personality, vocabulary, and rhetorical devices are identical. What scales up:

- **Pacing** — articles develop arguments across sections; posts compress them into paragraphs
- **Evidence** — articles cite research and data; posts cite experience
- **Structure** — articles use section headers; posts use whitespace
- **Length** — 1,000–3,500 words (5–15 min read). Let the argument dictate length. Never pad.

### Title and subtitle

Every article has both. The title is a claim or question. The subtitle is one sentence that elaborates or reframes.

Examples from existing articles:
- "AI Can Write Code. Coordinating AI Agents Is the Actual Hard Part." / "Building a full-stack SaaS platform with a 12-agent orchestration system using Claude Code custom agents."
- "Is SaaS Dead?" / "No. But the version of SaaS most people are selling might be."
- "Pain Gets You In The Door. Curiosity Builds Everything Else" / "Why organizations keep treating AI adoption as a pain problem — and why that's only half the answer."

### Opening patterns

Start with a scene, a thesis, or a provocation. Never a generic framing.

- **Specific scenario:** "I watched it happen on a Tuesday afternoon. A project manager agent read a feature spec, spawned three planning agents in parallel..." — Drop the reader into a moment.
- **Declarative reframe:** "Here's what happened. AI made coding cheap. Not free, not effortless, but cheap enough that the build vs. buy equation flipped." — State what changed, plainly.
- **Provocative question answered immediately:** Title asks the question, first paragraph answers it, rest of article explains why. "Is SaaS Dead? No. But..."

What NOT to do: formatted title cards, "In today's rapidly evolving landscape...", abstract scene-setting that delays the point.

### Section headers

Short, thematic, often questions or declarations. They signal a gear shift, not just structure.

Good: "What actually dies", "The uncomfortable part", "The inversion", "The Context Window Trap", "Back to the annoying CEO"
Bad: "Introduction", "Background", "Analysis", "Conclusion", "Key Takeaways"

Use 4–7 sections for a typical article. Numbered sections only when the piece is a walkthrough or tutorial (like the AI agents article).

### Closing pattern

Return to the opening. Reframe it with everything the reader now knows. End with a punchy 1–2 line summary.

Examples:
- Opens with chaotic AI agents → closes with "The next time you watch an AI agent generate code, ask yourself: who reviews this?"
- Opens with the CEO checking AI → closes with "Pain is the ignition. Curiosity is the engine. You need both. But only one of them scales."
- Opens with "the math changed" → closes with "The technology gets commoditized. The person who knows how to use it doesn't."

The last line should make the reader reconsider the entire piece. Same device as LinkedIn posts, just earned over more paragraphs.

### Evidence and citations

Cite research inline, conversationally. Not footnotes, not academic format.

Good: "According to McKinsey's 2025 State of AI report, nearly 90% of organizations now use AI regularly in at least one function."
Good: "A 2026 study published in Frontiers in Psychology identified intrinsic motivation as the strongest single predictor of creative and frequent AI use."

3–5 sources per article is the sweet spot. Enough to ground the argument, not so many it reads like a literature review.

Use data to set up a tension ("90% adoption" vs "7% scaling"), not to prove an obvious point.

### Pacing and structure

Each section earns its place. The argument should develop, not just list points.

Pattern that works: introduce a tension → explore one side → explore the other → complicate it → resolve or reframe.

Use single-line paragraphs as emphasis beats (same as LinkedIn posts). A standalone sentence after a longer block creates a pause.

Let sections breathe. A section can be 150 words or 500 words depending on what the argument needs. Don't force uniformity.

### Diagrams and visuals

Use when they clarify a concept that words alone make clunky. The existing articles use:
- 2x2 matrices for categorization (Pain vs Curiosity)
- Before/after comparison diagrams (Build vs Buy cost shift)
- Flow diagrams for processes (Motivation Internalization Pathway)
- Conceptual illustrations for hero images

Style: clean, minimal, light backgrounds. Text readable. No decorative flourishes.

### What to avoid in articles

- Bullet-point lists as a substitute for argument development. Use them sparingly for actual lists (agent roster, technical specs), never for the core argument.
- "Key takeaways" or "TL;DR" sections. The article IS the takeaway.
- Transition phrases that announce the structure: "Now let's turn to...", "As we discussed above...", "In the next section..."
- Generic examples when you have specific ones from your own experience.
- Hedging the thesis. Take a position. Complicate it later, but start with a clear claim.
