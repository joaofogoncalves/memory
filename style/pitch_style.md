## Self-Positioning Style Guide

Use this when writing artifacts where João is the subject — the About page, cover letters, recruiter outreach, interview prep, elevator pitches, talk abstracts, speaker bios. This is the *self-positioning* mode.

`writing_style.md` is for LinkedIn posts. `article_style.md` is for essays. This document covers what's different when the topic is *you*.

It applies to skills that consume it — `/cover-letter`, `/outreach`, `/interview-prep`, `/pitch` — and to manual writing of the About page or anything else that pitches the person rather than the work.

---

## Voice when writing about yourself

Same operator voice as the articles and posts. Confidence through evidence, not through adjectives. You are an engineering leader and AI practitioner. You've shipped the thing. You're not theorizing.

- **No hedging flourishes.** "I'd love to bring my passion for…" is dead on arrival. Replace with: "I built X. It did Y." The receipt is the qualification.
- **No humility theatre.** Don't downplay scope ("just a small project") and don't overclaim ("transformed the industry"). State the size and shape and let it land.
- **Insider-turned-something framing where it fits.** "I've built these systems. I know what they look like from the inside" is a stronger move than "I have experience in." Use it where it's true.
- **Specificity over abstraction.** Dates, deltas, named systems beat adjectives. "Built a 14-agent orchestration system at BRIDGE IN" beats "built AI systems." "Drove a $20M acquisition" beats "led strategic initiatives."
- **You're allowed to have a take.** Self-positioning artifacts that read like a CV with a personality grafted on read worse than ones with a clear point of view. If you have a thesis about how engineering or AI works, lead with it.

## The proof-first opener

Every self-positioning artifact opens with a specific proof point. Not a framing. Not an introduction. A thing you did.

- **Cover letter** — open with a proof point that maps to the role. Not "I'm writing to apply for X" or "I came across this role and was excited to…"
- **Recruiter DM** — open with the relevance, not the request. "I run the engineering function at a 14-agent AI orchestration system" beats "I'd love to chat about opportunities."
- **Elevator pitch** — open with what you're doing now, in one sentence with a number in it. "I'm a Founding Engineer at BRIDGE IN running a 14-agent AI orchestration system" beats "I'm an engineering leader with 15+ years of experience."
- **About page hero** — same. State the thing in one line. Let the reader earn the context.

What the opener should have:
- A number or named system
- Either present-tense ("I run") or recent-past ("I built") — not "experienced in"
- No positioning language ("strategic," "innovative," "passionate")

## Lead with proof, not promises

This is the operating principle. Borrowed from James Gardner's "817 Applications" site. It applies to every artifact.

| Promise (don't write this) | Proof (write this) |
|---|---|
| "I would bring deep expertise in scaling teams." | "I scaled engineering from 95 to 200 in 18 months at Planday." |
| "I'm passionate about AI-augmented engineering." | "I run a 14-agent orchestration system that ships full-stack features end-to-end." |
| "I have a track record of driving outcomes." | "Drove a $20M acquisition by Altium. Retained 90% of staff post-merger." |
| "Strong leadership skills." | "Coached three new team leads through a post-acquisition org redesign." |
| "Excellent at problem-solving." | (delete; show it in the next bullet) |

The promise pattern is unfalsifiable. Anyone can claim it. The proof pattern names a thing that either happened or didn't. Always pick the proof.

When you can't replace a promise with a proof, delete it. A short, dense artifact that's all proof beats a long one padded with promises.

## Quiet ask, not marketing CTA

Soft-hunt voice. Closes invite a conversation; they don't sell.

**Don't write:**
- "Let's connect!"
- "I'd love to discuss further."
- "Looking forward to hearing from you!"
- "Hit me up — I'd love to chat!"
- "Excited to learn more about this opportunity."

**Do write (cover letter / email):**
- "If that's the kind of problem you're hiring against, happy to talk."
- "Worth a 20-minute call if you're sizing the role this quarter?"
- A specific question about the role: "How are you currently splitting work between humans and agents on the eng team?"

**Do write (About page CTA):**
- "If you're building something at the edge of agents and engineering, start a conversation."
- "Open to: founding/early-stage engineering roles, fractional eng leadership, advisory work with operators."

The closer should sound like a real person extending a real invitation, not a marketer asking for a click.

## Length targets per surface

These are guidelines, not laws. Cut anything that doesn't earn its place.

| Surface | Target | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| About page hero headline | 1 line, 12 words max | Stand-alone claim. No subordinate clauses. |
| About page section | 2–3 short paragraphs per section | 50–150 words each. Italic concept sub-headline optional. |
| Cover letter | 250–350 words | Three paragraphs: proof opener / specific fit / quiet ask. Don't fill the page. |
| LinkedIn DM (recruiter / HM) | 60–90 words | Hook-first. No link. One question. |
| Email cold (recruiter / HM / founder) | 130–180 words | Three short paragraphs. Site link OK. |
| Outreach follow-up | 30–60 words | One nudge. No guilt. |
| Elevator 30s | ~75 words | One thing you do, one number, what you're looking for. |
| Elevator 60s | ~150 words | Add one specific story or accomplishment. |
| Elevator 2min | ~300 words | Add the thesis and the through-line across roles. |
| LinkedIn headline | ≤160 chars | One claim, one position, one keyword for search. |
| LinkedIn About summary | 1,500–2,500 chars | First 220 chars are the hook. Lead with proof. |
| Talk abstract | 200–300 words | Title + one-sentence thesis + 2 paragraphs + bullet of takeaways. |
| Speaker bio (short) | ~50 words | One sentence: name, role, one notable. |
| Speaker bio (medium) | ~100 words | Add one previous role and the through-line. |
| Speaker bio (long) | ~200 words | Add the thesis and one credit/badge that's relevant. |

## Format hygiene

- **No em-dashes in body text.** Same rule as `writing_style.md`. Use commas, periods, or sentence breaks. The `BRIDGE IN — Founding Engineer` style header is fine; em-dashes inside sentences are not.
- **No LinkedIn-corporate phrases.** "Leverage," "synergy," "passionate about," "driven by," "thought leader," "results-oriented," "best-in-class," "strategic vision," "actionable insights." Cut on sight.
- **No filler AI phrases.** "It's worth noting," "in today's landscape," "at the end of the day," "in the ever-evolving world of."
- **Plain words over fancy ones.** "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate." "Build" not "architect" (when the thing is just building, not architecting).
- **No fake bullets in prose.** If something is a list, make it a list. If it's a paragraph, make it a paragraph. Don't fake-bullet inside a sentence with "X, Y, and Z" when the items deserve their own lines.
- **Avoid "I'm a X with Y years of experience…"** It's the most-written first sentence in CV summaries. Replace with what you do, not what you've accumulated.

## Specificity rules

The pattern that travels:

- **Numbers > adjectives.** "Scaled 95 → 200" beats "scaled significantly."
- **Named systems > generic categories.** "Django + React + 14-agent orchestration" beats "modern web stack."
- **Dates > durations.** "Aug 2022 to Feb 2024" beats "for 18 months."
- **Verbs that did a thing > verbs that signal seniority.** "Built," "shipped," "wrote," "ran," "decided" beat "led," "managed," "oversaw," "drove" — though the latter are fine when the action really was leadership.
- **Outcome > activity.** "Cut deploy time 88%" beats "led DevOps transformation."

When you have a number, use it exactly once and don't dilute it. "Scaled 95 → 200" is one usage. "Scaled the team significantly, going from 95 people to over 200 across the company" is dilution masquerading as detail.

## The James moves (patterns that travel)

From `https://james-hiring-insight.netlify.app/`. These work whenever the artifact is allowed to be a single argument rather than a list of bullets:

- **Numbered sections** (`01`, `02`, `03`) for self-positioning long-form. Works on the About page; can work on a multi-section cover letter for senior roles.
- **Italic concept sub-headlines** under each section header. ("they're broken", "the numbers don't lie".) Use sparingly; one per section max.
- **Imperative + noun** for any prescriptive line. ("Lead with proof", not "How to lead with proof".)
- **Metric pills** under career entries — small, mono, muted, separated by middots. Lets the prose stay light while the receipts stay loud.
- **Symmetric prescriptions.** When you have advice for two audiences, mirror the structure (3 for one, 3 for the other, same shape).
- **Closing reframe.** End where you started, but with everything the reader now knows. The last line should make the artifact land twice.
- **The credentialed admission.** "I've built these systems. I know how they work. And still…" — works when there's a tension worth surfacing.

## Anti-pattern checklist

Before shipping a self-positioning artifact, verify none of these are present:

- [ ] No "passionate about"
- [ ] No "results-driven" / "results-oriented"
- [ ] No "leverage" as a verb
- [ ] No "thought leader"
- [ ] No "in today's [adjective] landscape"
- [ ] No "Let's connect!" or "Looking forward to hearing from you!"
- [ ] No paragraph that's a promise without a proof
- [ ] No opener that's an introduction instead of a proof point
- [ ] No "I'd be a great fit because…"
- [ ] No em-dashes inside sentences
- [ ] No emoji as bullet markers
- [ ] No formatted title at the top of a cover letter ("Application for…")

If any of these are present, rewrite. The voice is broken the moment they appear.
