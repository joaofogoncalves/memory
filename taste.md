# Visual Taste Profile

You share images on LinkedIn that follow these patterns. Use this guide when selecting or creating images for posts.

## Image Types

Default to one of these five categories. Roughly weighted by frequency in current voice:

1. **Dark-mode X/Twitter screenshot of a single post** — about half of all images. Used as the reaction-anchor whenever the post is a take on someone else's claim. Crop tight, no surrounding UI chrome, no browser frame.
2. **Custom dark-mode diagram in your own brand palette** — second most common. Deep navy (#0e131e-adjacent) background, teal accents (#44d8f1-adjacent), mono + sans typography, "JG joaofogoncalves.com" wordmark bottom right. Used for original ideas: comparison tables, two-column "before/after" frames, arrow diagrams, ladders, side-by-side concept maps.
3. **External chart or data graphic in its native palette** — kept untouched. Lenny's job-market chart, Anthropic's radar charts, Google Cloud Next slide, BullshitBench leaderboards, adoption-ladder diagrams. Don't restyle them.
4. **Candid event photography** — documentary, no branded overlay. Wide stage shots, audience POV, close-ups of collaborators looking at laptops. Soft daylight or natural venue light.
5. **Painterly editorial article hero** — only for article-promotion posts. Cinematic, off-palette (warm tones, muted moody scenes), Wired/NYT-Magazine register. Bold sans-serif title overlaid, wordmark bottom right.

Rare and acceptable: light-mode screenshots from sources that are natively light-mode (Substack, Reddit, Anthropic announcement cards). Don't force dark mode on light-mode source material.

## Visual Composition

- Default to **dark mode**. Custom diagrams, screenshots, and product UI are all dark.
- Crop **tight**. Single subject per image — one tweet, one chart, one stage photo. No collages, no side-by-side panels of unrelated content.
- Keep **subjects centered with breathing room**. Site templates crop thumbnails at multiple aspect ratios; assume the corners may be cut.
- **Text density** runs medium-to-high on diagrams and screenshots, low on event photography and article heroes. Match density to type, never split the difference.
- Custom diagrams use **monospace for code/IDs and sans for headings**. No serif typography in custom assets.
- Use **one teal accent** per diagram for emphasis (a single highlighted node, one ascending line, one selected card). Never paint everything in teal.

## Subject Matter

- Screenshots almost always show **a single take from a named, identifiable account** — researchers, founders, journalists, CEOs, dev-tool builders. Treat the screenshot as a quote you're reacting to, not as decoration.
- Custom diagrams visualize the **specific thesis** of the post — over-engineering vs over-agenting, AI-first vs AI-assisted, pipeline vs nervous system, bottleneck-moved, AI-authorship-curve. The diagram is the post's punchline rendered as shape.
- Event photos document **where you actually were**: Agents Day, AIhub Innovation Day, juries, conferences. Never use stock event photography. If you weren't there, there's no event photo.
- Article heroes carry the **mood of the essay**, not the brand of the site. Canyons for "Great Filter," turnstiles for the job-market piece, painterly editorial scenes for systems essays.

## Platform and Source Patterns

- **X (Twitter) is the dominant screenshot source.** When quoting a take, default to the X version of the post over LinkedIn's UI even if you also saw it on LinkedIn.
- **Anthropic, Cloudflare, Lenny Rachitsky, Substack, Google** are recurring sources. Their native chrome is fine to include if it identifies the source clearly.
- Recurring quoted accounts: @yacineMTB (kache), Yegor Bugayenko, Matt Gray, Kyle Gawley, Lenny Rachitsky, Filip Kowalski, John Graham-Cumming, Peter Gostev. The information diet is X-heavy, Substack-second.
- **No LinkedIn screenshot of your own posts.** The site is the canonical home; don't recursively quote your LinkedIn UI.

## Branding and Consistency

- Custom diagrams are **fully branded**: navy + teal, mono + sans, JG wordmark. This is your "studio identity" and it should be instantly recognizable.
- Screenshots and event photos are **unbranded**. No watermark, no overlay, no JG wordmark applied retroactively. The source's native presentation is the point.
- Article heroes are **deliberately off-brand**: they carry the essay's mood, not the site's palette. The only brand element is the wordmark in the bottom corner, small and unobtrusive.
- Consistency level: **curated and intentional**. Each image type has its own discipline. Don't mix — never restyle a screenshot, never paint an article hero in site palette, never brand an event photo.

## What NOT To Share

- **No generic stock photography.** No suit-with-glowing-brain, no diverse-team-pointing-at-laptop, no abstract neural-network background.
- **No AI-generated illustrations except for editorial article heroes.** If you do use one, it must look painterly and cinematic — not Midjourney-cyberpunk, not glossy-3D-render, not the default LinkedIn-AI-art aesthetic.
- **No motivational quote cards.** No quote-on-gradient. No Canva templates. No "leadership lesson" tiles.
- **No screenshots of your own LinkedIn UI** — even when referencing reactions or comments. The voice never reflects back on itself through LinkedIn's frame.
- **No emoji-heavy or sticker-heavy graphics.** No reaction-meme overlays. No "🚀💡🔥" composites.
- **No collages or grids of unrelated content.** One subject per image.
- **No light-mode versions of inherently dark sources.** Don't toggle a dark-mode tweet into a light-mode card.
- **No screenshots with surrounding browser chrome, tab bars, or notification overlays.** Crop to the content.
- **No on-brand restyling of external charts.** If it's someone else's chart, share it as-is.
- **No "polished" infographic with five points and icons.** Custom diagrams should be sparse — one or two concepts, one accent color, the shape doing the work.
