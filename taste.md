# Visual Taste Profile

You share images on LinkedIn that follow these patterns. Use this guide when selecting or creating images for posts.

## Image Types

Your recent posts heavily favor two types: screenshots (~40%) and AI-generated conceptual illustrations (~35%). Diagrams and infographics make up ~19%. Real photographs have disappeared entirely from recent content.

- Default to screenshots of real conversations, tweets, or product UIs when commenting on industry discourse
- Use AI-generated illustrations only for abstract concepts that need a visual anchor (e.g., "what AI agents look like")
- Use diagrams and infographics when explaining frameworks, matrices, or data-driven arguments
- Share real photographs only for genuine personal moments (events, books, office life) -- these are rare and should stay rare

Historical note: earlier posts (2024) heavily used AI-generated split-layout banners (title left, illustration right), hand-drawn sketches, whimsical cartoons, editorial cartoons, and decorative 3D illustrations. These have been phased out in favor of screenshots and evidence-based visuals.

## Visual Composition

- Text density skews heavy -- most images carry readable text (quotes, titles, data labels)
- Split-layout banners (text left, illustration right) were the dominant format historically (~60% of 2024 posts) but have been almost entirely replaced
- Dark mode dominates recent screenshots and graphics (~35% of all images use dark backgrounds)
- When using color, lean toward deep blues, teals, and dark navy over bright pastels
- Full-width screenshots are preferred over cropped sections

## Subject Matter

Every image should be load-bearing -- it must reinforce the post's argument, not just decorate it.

- AI, engineering, and leadership are the three visual pillars (each ~35% of images)
- Screenshots of industry voices (tech CEOs, engineers, thought leaders) serve as evidence or springboards
- Diagrams visualize specific frameworks referenced in the text (Stacey Matrix, Hype Cycle, Spotify Model, etc.)
- When sharing data, use the original source chart (Stack Overflow, Gartner, Anthropic) rather than recreating it
- Book photos work when they're personal (your copy, with sticky tabs) -- not stock images of book covers

## Platform and Source Patterns

- Twitter/X screenshots are the dominant source (~68% of all screenshots), always in dark mode
- Prefer screenshots of verified thought leaders, witty one-liners, or sharp takes over long threads
- Tumblr, Reddit, and iMessage screenshots appear occasionally for humor -- keep these rare and high-signal
- Course/curriculum screenshots work for educational resource shares
- Product dashboards and terminal screenshots work for technical demonstrations

## Branding and Consistency

- No rigid visual template -- the brand is "substance over polish"
- Consistency comes from tone (professional, opinionated, evidence-based) not from visual uniformity
- BRIDGE IN branding appears naturally in company-related posts but is never forced
- Code and terminal imagery (~23% of images) is a recurring element that reinforces technical credibility
- Friendly robot/AI agent illustrations appear in AI-focused posts -- keep them non-threatening and stylized

## What NOT To Share

- Generic stock photos of "diverse happy workers" or "person at whiteboard" -- these read as filler
- Overly polished, corporate-style banner graphics with gradient backgrounds and buzzword titles (e.g., "EMBRACING INNOVATION AND DATA-DRIVEN CULTURE FOR SUCCESS" -- this was the 2024 voice, not the current one)
- AI-generated 3D illustrations of floating icons, glowing screens, or abstract tech elements (used historically, now off-brand)
- Split-layout banners with AI illustration on one side and motivational title on the other -- this was the dominant 2024 format and reads as dated now
- Hand-drawn/whiteboard-style sketches unless sourcing a specific well-known framework
- Purely decorative images with no connection to the post's argument
- Bright whimsical color palettes (pastels, pinks, magentas) -- the current aesthetic is darker and more grounded
- Personal lifestyle photos (running stats, vacation snaps) unless there's a genuine professional tie-in
- Quote cards with stock portrait photos -- if quoting someone, screenshot the original post instead
