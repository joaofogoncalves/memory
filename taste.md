# Visual Taste Profile

You share images on LinkedIn that follow these patterns. Use this guide when selecting or creating images for posts.

## Image Types

Your most effective format is **screenshots of tweets/posts** (~35% of images). These are real people saying real things — they serve as evidence for your commentary and feel authentic.

- Twitter/X screenshots: 35% — your signature format, almost always in dark mode
- AI-generated conceptual art: 26% — used as thumbnail/hero images for opinion posts
- Data visualizations and charts: 10% — from newsletters, research reports, benchmarks
- Product/tool screenshots: 6% — Claude Code UI, terminal sessions
- Infographics: 6% — third-party cheat sheets, branded tool documentation
- Quote cards, editorial photos, comics, stat graphics: remaining ~17%

Prioritize screenshots and data over conceptual art. Your strongest posts pair a real screenshot with sharp commentary.

## Visual Composition

- **Text-heavy images dominate.** Most of your images contain readable text — tweets, code, stats, charts with labels. Your audience reads the image, then reads your take.
- **Dark mode is your default.** ~65% of images have dark backgrounds. This reflects your actual tool usage (X/Twitter dark mode, terminal, Claude UI).
- **Tight crops work well.** Your best screenshots are cropped to just the relevant content — a single tweet, a usage meter, a chart. No excess UI chrome.
- **Square and landscape formats.** Avoid tall portrait images. Your images sit well in LinkedIn's feed as landscape or roughly square.

## Subject Matter

Every image connects to AI, software engineering, or tech leadership. The relationship to your post text falls into clear patterns:

- **Evidence images**: A tweet or data point that proves your argument. You quote it, then add your perspective. This is your strongest pattern.
- **Reaction images**: Someone else's hot take that you respond to — agreeing, disagreeing, or adding nuance.
- **Conceptual thumbnails**: AI-generated or stock art that illustrates the post's theme (doors, robots, multi-armed figures). These are your weakest images — they add atmosphere but not information.
- **Product screenshots**: Your own tool usage (Claude Code terminal, usage dashboards). These feel genuine and differentiate your posts.

## Platform and Source Patterns

- **X/Twitter dark mode** is your primary screenshot source. Share tweets from tech leaders, developers, and industry voices (Boris Cherny, Uncle Bob Martin, Cody Plofker, Aaron Francis, Malte Ubl).
- **Newsletter charts** from Lenny Rachitsky / TrueUp, Anthropic research reports, BullshitBench. Clean data visualization on white or warm backgrounds.
- **Claude Code UI**: Dark terminal screenshots showing real usage — multi-agent sessions, usage limits. These are uniquely yours.
- **Ed Sim / Substack quote cards**: Minimal white background with large text quotes from newsletters.

## Branding and Consistency

- **No consistent template or personal branding.** Your images are organic and varied — each sourced or created for the specific post.
- **Authenticity over polish.** Screenshots of real tools, real tweets, real data. This matches your voice.
- **When you use AI-generated art**, it tends toward dark/moody/neon aesthetics (purple circuitry, glowing doors, cyberpunk silhouettes). These are recognizable but generic.
- **Your most distinctive images** are product screenshots and developer tool UIs — things only someone actually using these tools would share.

## What NOT To Share

- **Generic AI-generated hero images** with neon circuits, multi-armed silhouettes, or robots at desks. You use these, but they're your least distinctive content. They look like every other AI-enthusiast post on LinkedIn. Use sparingly.
- **Stock photos** of people in offices, handshakes, or generic "teamwork" imagery. You never use these and shouldn't start.
- **Polished branded graphics** with gradients and marketing copy. Your audience responds to raw, real content.
- **Carousel-style formatted posts** with designed slides. Your voice works best with a single powerful image plus text.
- **Low-resolution or tiny images** that can't be read in the LinkedIn feed. If sharing a tweet, make sure the text is legible.
- **Images without a clear connection to your text.** Every image should either be evidence (screenshot, chart) or illustration (conceptual). If it's neither, skip the image.
